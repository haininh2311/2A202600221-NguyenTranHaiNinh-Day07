# Debt Management: Understanding and Eliminating Debt

## Good Debt vs Bad Debt

Not all debt is created equal. Understanding the difference between productive debt and destructive debt helps you make better borrowing decisions.

**Good debt** finances an asset that appreciates in value or increases your earning power. A student loan used to complete a high-return degree is an example. A mortgage that builds home equity over time is another. Good debt typically has lower interest rates and tax advantages.

**Bad debt** finances depreciating assets or consumable experiences. Credit card debt used for dining, shopping, or vacations is the clearest example. Interest rates on credit cards commonly range from 18–28% per year, meaning the true cost of that $100 dinner could become $130 if you take a year to pay it off.

## Student Loans

Student loans are often a student's first and largest debt. Before borrowing, calculate your expected return on investment: if a degree costs $50,000 in total debt, the resulting salary increase should justify that cost.

**Federal vs Private Loans:** Federal student loans offer income-driven repayment plans, deferment options, and potential forgiveness programs. Private loans from banks or credit unions typically have fewer protections and less flexible repayment terms. Always exhaust federal loan options before turning to private loans.

**Capitalized Interest:** During deferment, interest continues to accrue on unsubsidized loans. When repayment begins, unpaid interest is added to the principal balance — a process called capitalization. This can significantly increase the total amount you owe. Making small interest payments during school prevents capitalization.

## The Debt Avalanche Method

The debt avalanche method prioritizes paying off the debt with the highest interest rate first while making minimum payments on all others. Once the highest-rate debt is paid off, redirect its payment toward the next highest-rate debt. This method minimizes total interest paid over time and is mathematically optimal.

Example: You have three debts — a credit card at 24%, a personal loan at 12%, and a student loan at 5%. Pay any extra money toward the credit card first, regardless of balance size.

## The Debt Snowball Method

The debt snowball method prioritizes paying off the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. The psychological win of eliminating a debt entirely provides motivation to continue. Research shows that this method, though not mathematically optimal, leads to higher debt repayment completion rates for people who struggle with motivation.

## Credit Scores

A credit score (typically 300–850 in the US) measures your creditworthiness. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve loans and at what interest rate. A higher score means lower interest rates, saving thousands of dollars over a lifetime.

The five factors affecting your credit score: payment history (35%), credit utilization (30%), length of credit history (15%), credit mix (10%), and new credit inquiries (10%). Paying every bill on time and keeping credit card balances below 30% of your limit are the two highest-impact actions you can take.
