# Investing Basics: Growing Your Money Over Time

## Why Invest?

Saving money in a bank account protects your principal but loses purchasing power to inflation over time. Investing puts your money to work by buying assets that can grow in value. Historically, the US stock market has returned an average of 7–10% per year after inflation, far outpacing the 0.5–2% typical savings account interest rate.

The core principle of investing is that **higher potential returns come with higher risk**. Understanding this trade-off is the starting point for every investment decision.

## Asset Classes

**Stocks (Equities):** Buying a share of stock means buying a small ownership stake in a company. If the company grows and becomes more valuable, your share price rises. Stocks have the highest long-term return potential but also the greatest short-term volatility. A stock portfolio can lose 30–50% of its value in a market downturn.

**Bonds (Fixed Income):** Bonds are loans you make to governments or corporations in exchange for regular interest payments and the return of principal at maturity. Bonds are more stable than stocks but offer lower returns. They serve as a stabilizing component in a diversified portfolio.

**Index Funds and ETFs:** An index fund pools money from many investors to buy all stocks in a market index, such as the S&P 500. This provides instant diversification. Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) work similarly but trade on exchanges like individual stocks. Index funds are the recommended starting point for new investors because they offer broad market exposure at very low cost.

## Compound Interest: The Most Powerful Force in Investing

Compound interest means earning returns on your returns. If you invest $1,000 at 7% annual return, after one year you have $1,070. In year two, you earn 7% on $1,070 — not just the original $1,000. Over 30 years, that initial $1,000 grows to over $7,600 without adding another dollar.

Starting early has an outsized impact on outcomes. Investing $200 per month from age 22 to 30 (8 years, $19,200 total) and then stopping will often outperform someone who invests $200 per month from age 30 to 60 (30 years, $72,000 total), because of the longer compound period.

## Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across different asset types, industries, and geographies to reduce risk. When one sector underperforms, others may compensate. A globally diversified portfolio — holding US stocks, international stocks, and bonds — has historically provided strong returns with lower volatility than any single asset class.

## Common Investing Mistakes

**Market timing:** Trying to buy at the lowest point and sell at the highest is nearly impossible even for professionals. Consistent investing regardless of market conditions (dollar-cost averaging) outperforms market timing for most investors.

**Panic selling:** Selling investments during a market downturn locks in losses. Historical data shows that staying invested through crashes and recoveries produces better outcomes than selling and waiting.

**High fees:** A fund with 1% annual fee versus 0.05% costs you roughly 20% of your total wealth over 30 years. Always check expense ratios before investing.
