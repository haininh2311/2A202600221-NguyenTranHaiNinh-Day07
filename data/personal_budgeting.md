# Personal Budgeting for Students

## What Is a Budget?

A budget is a plan that tracks your income and expenses over a fixed period, typically one month. Creating a budget helps you understand exactly where your money goes and ensures you spend less than you earn. Without a budget, it is easy to overspend without realizing it until your bank account runs dry.

## The 50/30/20 Rule

The 50/30/20 rule is one of the most popular budgeting frameworks for beginners. It divides your after-tax income into three categories: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings and debt repayment.

**Needs (50%):** Essential expenses you cannot avoid — rent, groceries, utilities, transportation to school or work, and minimum loan payments.

**Wants (30%):** Non-essential spending that improves your quality of life — dining out, subscriptions like Netflix or Spotify, hobbies, and entertainment.

**Savings and Debt (20%):** This portion goes toward building your emergency fund, investing for the future, and paying down high-interest debt faster than the minimum required.

## Zero-Based Budgeting

Zero-based budgeting assigns every dollar of your income to a specific category until your budget equals zero. You are not spending every dollar; you are *planning* every dollar. Any money without a category becomes savings or extra debt payment. This method forces intentionality and prevents money from disappearing without explanation.

## How to Create Your First Budget

**Step 1 — Calculate your total monthly income.** Include salary, freelance work, scholarships, parental support, and any other consistent source.

**Step 2 — List all fixed expenses.** These are costs that stay the same each month: rent, insurance premiums, loan minimums, and subscriptions.

**Step 3 — Estimate variable expenses.** These change monthly: food, transport, entertainment, and personal care. Review your last three months of bank statements to find realistic averages.

**Step 4 — Subtract total expenses from income.** If the result is positive, allocate the surplus to savings or debt. If negative, identify which want categories to cut.

**Step 5 — Track and adjust weekly.** A budget is only useful if you follow it. Use a spreadsheet, a notes app, or a budgeting app like YNAB or Mint to log every transaction.

## Common Budgeting Mistakes

Forgetting irregular expenses (annual insurance, semester fees, car registration) is one of the biggest budgeting mistakes. Divide annual costs by 12 and set aside that amount monthly. Another common mistake is creating an unrealistic budget — if you budget zero for fun, you will likely abandon the entire plan within a week.
